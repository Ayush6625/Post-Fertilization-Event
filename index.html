Post Fertilization

Post-fertilization events are the processes that occur after the fusion of the male and female gametes during sexual reproduction. These events are crucial for the development of the zygote into a mature seed or fruit. Post-fertilization events are broadly divided into two main processes: Endosperm formation and embryogeny

What is Post-Fertilization?

Events that take place after the zygote formation in sexual reproduction are known as Post-Fertilization Events. The post-fertilization process is critical in ensuring the embryo’s survival and development, which ultimately results in the birth of healthy offspring. Various cellular and molecular events occur during this process, involving the activation of specific genes and signaling pathways, ultimately leading to the differentiation and specialization of the various cell types needed for embryo development. Endosperm formation is the process of forming the endosperm, which is a nutritive tissue that provides nourishment to the developing embryo. The endosperm is formed by the fusion of two polar nuclei with a sperm cell, resulting in a triploid nucleus. This triploid nucleus divides and develops into a tissue that surrounds the embryo, providing nutrients for its growth. Embryogeny is the process of forming the embryo, which is the immature plant that develops from the zygote. During embryogeny, the zygote undergoes several rounds of cell division and differentiation, resulting in the formation of different tissues and organs of the developing plant. These include the shoot, root, cotyledons, and embryonic axis.

The Zygote

In all sexually reproducing species, the creation of a diploid zygote is a crucial step. The zygote is created in the surrounding environment, often water, in species that undergo external fertilizationexternal fertilization. In contrast, the zygote is created within the organism’s body in animals that undergo internal fertilization. The zygote’s future development is determined by the organism’s life cycle and the environmental factors it experiences. In the case of fungi and algae, the zygote forms a tough outer shell to defend itself from desiccation and injury. The zygote germinates after a period of hibernation. For organisms with a haplontic life cycle, meiosis produces haploid spores, which grow into haploid individuals. The zygote is the crucial connection that promotes species continuation between organisms of one generation and the next. Every sexually reproducing organism, including humans, begins as a single cell called a zygote.

Embryogenesis

Embryogenesis is the process through which an embryo develops from a zygote. The zygote goes through mitosis and cell differentiation during development. While cell division increases the number of cells in the developing embryo, cell differentiation allows groupings of cells to undergo specific alterations in order to become specialized tissues and organs that eventually form an organism. Animals can be classified as either oviparous or viviparous based on whether their zygotes develop outside or inside the female parent’s body. Oviparous animals, such as reptiles and birds, lay fertilized or unfertilized eggs covered by a hard shell in a safe environment. These eggs undergo incubation, and eventually, the young ones hatch out of the eggs. On the contrary, viviparous animals, including most mammals, including humans, carry their young ones inside their bodies until they reach a certain stage of growth. Afterward, they give birth to their young ones. Due to proper embryonic care and protection, the survival rate of the offspring is higher in viviparous organisms.
